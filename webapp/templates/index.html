How to Read This Map

What am I looking at?

This interactive map shows how a machine learning model organizes microscopy experiments based on the visual characteristics of the cells.

Each point represents one experimental well. Wells that appear close together contain cells with similar visual patterns according to the model, while wells farther apart have more different learned representations.

The map is a two-dimensional visualization of a much larger feature space. The goal is to help explore patterns in the model's learned representation of cellular morphology.

What does each point represent?

Each point represents a single well from a Cell Painting experiment.

A physical plate holds many individual wells arranged in a grid — each well is one independent mini-experiment (one compound, imaged separately). Every point on the map corresponds to exactly one well.

Each well can hold a different treatment (color) — one well, zoomed in, shows multiple images, each with many cells.

Each well contains many cells and microscopy images. The model first extracts a visual representation from those images, then summarizes them into a single embedding that represents the overall cellular phenotype observed in that well.

The model converts the microscopy images from each well into a numerical representation called an embedding — a compact "fingerprint" of the cell images that captures important visual features learned by the model.

In this visualization, each well is represented by a 384-dimensional embedding that has been projected into two dimensions for visualization.

Learn more: Introduction to embeddings (TensorFlow)

What does distance mean?

The distance between points represents similarity in the model's learned representation.

  • Nearby points → cells with similar visual characteristics according to the model.
  • Distant points → cells with more different visual characteristics.

This similarity reflects phenotypic similarity — similarity in observable cellular appearance — and should not be interpreted as proof that two compounds have the same biological mechanism (the biological process a compound affects — for example, disrupting DNA replication or affecting a specific cellular pathway).

For example, two compounds may appear close together because they cause similar morphological changes, even if they act through different biological pathways.

How should I interpret clusters?

When several points form a visible group, it means the model's learned representation places those wells close together — they share similar visual patterns.

A cluster may suggest that the wells in it share a similar morphological response, which can be a useful starting point for a hypothesis. On its own, it does not prove that those wells share the same biological mechanism — confirming that requires independent biological validation.

What affects the map?

A well's position reflects a mix of influences. Genuine biological signal — differences in cell shape, organelle organization, and other morphological responses to a compound — is part of what the model has learned to represent, and is often the dominant reason two wells land near or far from each other.

Embeddings can also capture experimental variation: differences between plates, experiments, imaging sessions, or other technical conditions can influence where a well lands on the map, alongside genuine morphological differences.

For this reason, patterns on the map are best read as model-learned similarities rather than guaranteed biological relationships. Switching the "Color by" option to Plate is a quick way to check whether a given pattern lines up with plate identity rather than treatment.

Why are the axes labeled UMAP 1 and UMAP 2?

The original embeddings contain 384 dimensions, which cannot be directly visualized. UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) is a dimensionality reduction method that compresses high-dimensional data into two dimensions while attempting to preserve local relationships.

The UMAP axes themselves do not represent specific biological features. The meaningful information is:

  • which points are close together,
  • which groups form clusters,
  • and how samples relate to each other.

Learn more: UMAP documentation

How is similarity calculated?

Similarity between wells is calculated in the original, full-dimensional embedding space — not from the 2D positions shown on this map. See Technical Details for how that calculation works.

Why don't the closest points on the map always match the neighbor list?

The "Neighborhood Summary" and "Representative Neighbors" panels are computed directly in the full, 384-dimensional embedding space, not from the 2D positions shown on the map.

UMAP is built to preserve local structure, but compressing 384 dimensions down to 2 is lossy: a point can land visually near others on the map without being one of its true nearest neighbors in the full embedding space, and vice versa. This is an expected property of any 2D projection, not an error in the panel.

Two points close together in the full embedding space can land far apart after compressing to 2D — and an unrelated point can land right next to the selected one instead.

This is also what the dotted lines on the map itself are showing: click any point, and dotted lines are drawn from it to its true nearest neighbors — the same wells listed in the sidebar's "Nearest Neighbors" panel. A neighbor line that stretches far across the map, rather than staying short, is this exact effect made visible — that connected well really is a true neighbor in the full embedding space, even though the 2D layout happened to place it elsewhere.

What does the attention map show?

The attention map highlights which regions of the microscopy image the model relied on most when forming its representation of that well. Brighter regions received more emphasis; darker regions contributed less.

Attention is not a direct explanation of biology. It reflects what the model attended to, not what is biologically important — a highly attended region may or may not correspond to a meaningful cellular structure, and a biologically important structure will not always receive high attention.

About the Project

Project goal

This project tests whether a self-supervised vision model can learn useful representations of Cell Painting images without using compound or mechanism-of-action labels during training.

This visualization is a way to explore those learned representations directly — to see what structure the model found in the data on its own, and where that structure does and doesn't line up with known biology.

What is Cell Painting?

Cell Painting is a high-content microscopy assay that uses fluorescent stains to visualize multiple structures inside cells.

Cells are stained to highlight features including:

  • nucleus,
  • mitochondria,
  • endoplasmic reticulum,
  • RNA,
  • cytoskeletal and membrane structures.

A standard color photo has 3 channels — red, green, and blue — combined into one image. Cell Painting works the same way but with 5 channels instead of 3: each fluorescent stain is captured as its own separate grayscale image, one channel per structure above, rather than one combined color photo.

Each channel is its own grayscale image; the color on each edge here (and in the sidebar's channel bars) just identifies which stain it is.

Those 5 grayscale channels are what the model actually sees. The color composite shown in this app's thumbnails is only for human viewing — a distinct color is assigned to each channel afterward so the different structures can be told apart in one image.

By capturing many cellular features at once, Cell Painting provides a detailed view of how compounds change cell morphology.

Learn more: Broad Institute Cell Painting overview

Why analyze cell morphology?

Changes in a cell's appearance can reveal how a compound affects cellular processes.

Different perturbations may alter features such as:

  • cell shape,
  • organelle organization,
  • nuclear morphology,
  • texture,
  • and overall cellular state.

These observable characteristics are called phenotypes.

What did the model learn?

This project uses a self-supervised vision model called DINO (self-Distillation with No Labels).

Traditional supervised learning requires examples with known labels, such as: "This image came from a compound affecting pathway X."

Self-supervised learning instead allows a model to learn patterns directly from unlabeled data. During training, DINO learns a representation of microscopy images by identifying consistent visual patterns across different views of the same data.

Learn more: DINO: Emerging Properties in Self-Supervised Vision Transformers (arXiv)

Why is this approach interesting?

Large microscopy datasets contain enormous amounts of information, but manually labeling every image with biological annotations is expensive and incomplete.

Self-supervised learning provides a way to discover patterns without requiring predefined labels.

If wells with similar morphological responses cluster together after training, it suggests that the model has learned meaningful representations of cellular morphology.

What could this be used for?

If a model like this reliably captures phenotypic signal without labeled training data, it could support several practical uses in early-stage drug discovery:

  • Hit prioritization — surfacing which novel compounds look phenotypically similar to ones with a known mechanism.
  • Drug repurposing — flagging existing drugs whose morphological profile suggests an unexplored therapeutic use.
  • Mechanism discovery — generating hypotheses about an uncharacterized compound's mechanism by comparing it to better-understood compounds and genetic perturbations.
  • Prioritizing follow-up experiments — helping decide which hits are worth pursuing with more targeted, costly assays.

This project is a proof of concept for the first step in that pipeline — showing whether self-supervised learning can extract pharmacologically meaningful structure from Cell Painting images at all — not a deployed screening tool itself. See "Limitations" under Technical Details for where the current model falls short of that goal.

Dataset

This project uses data from the JUMP Cell Painting Consortium, an open resource containing large-scale Cell Painting microscopy experiments.

Specifically, it uses a subset of that resource focused on compound perturbation experiments — wells treated with a chemical compound, as opposed to genetic perturbations such as CRISPR knockouts or gene overexpression. The model is not trained on the full breadth of the JUMP Cell Painting dataset; current counts for this subset are shown in the "Dataset overview" panel.

Learn more: JUMP Cell Painting Consortium

Computational pipeline

The visualization was generated through the following pipeline:

Cell Painting microscopy images
Image preprocessing & quality control
DINO Vision Transformer
Learned image representations
Well-level aggregation
Embedding postprocessing
Similarity analysis
UMAP visualization

Training data

The model was trained on a subset of the JUMP Cell Painting dataset consisting of compound perturbation microscopy experiments — wells treated with a chemical compound rather than a genetic perturbation.

Training used no compound or mechanism-of-action labels: the goal was to learn morphology representations directly from the unlabeled images themselves.

Model architecture

The representation model is a Vision Transformer (ViT) trained using the DINO self-supervised learning framework.

A Vision Transformer divides images into smaller regions and learns relationships between those regions to build a representation of the image.

The model outputs a 384-dimensional embedding representing learned visual features.

Learn more: Vision Transformer paper (Google Research)

How is the model trained?

DINO trains with a self-distillation objective: a "student" network and a slower-moving "teacher" network (an exponential moving average of the student's own weights) are shown different augmented crops of the same image. The student is trained to match the teacher's output distribution across those different views using a cross-entropy loss, while the teacher's own outputs are centered and sharpened to prevent the trivial shortcut of every image collapsing to the same output.

Because both sides of this comparison come from the same unlabeled image, no compound or MoA label is ever involved in this training signal — see DINO (arXiv) for the full method.

From images to embeddings

Each well is imaged at multiple sites, producing several microscopy images per well. The model processes each image and produces a numerical feature representation for it.

Those per-image representations are then combined into a single well-level embedding, which is what gets postprocessed, compared for similarity, and ultimately plotted on the map.

Embeddings and similarity

An embedding is a numerical representation produced by the model.

Instead of comparing images directly pixel-by-pixel, embeddings allow the model to compare images based on learned features. For this project:

  • each well is represented by a 384-dimensional vector,
  • embeddings summarize learned morphological patterns,
  • similarity is measured using cosine similarity, which compares the orientation of two embedding vectors rather than their absolute scale — this is the calculation "How to Read This Map" refers back to.

Embedding processing

Before visualization, embeddings are processed to make them more comparable across plates and experiments. Processing includes:

  • median absolute deviation (MAD) normalization — reduces variation introduced by differences between plates and imaging batches,
  • sphering/whitening transformations — rebalances the feature space so no single learned pattern dominates comparisons,
  • normalization before similarity calculations — puts every embedding on the same scale so similarity scores are comparable across wells.

Together, these steps reduce unwanted technical variation and prepare embeddings for consistent similarity comparisons.

UMAP visualization

Because the embeddings exist in a 384-dimensional space, they cannot be directly displayed. UMAP creates a two-dimensional representation that preserves local relationships between samples as much as possible.

The final map is therefore an interactive window into the model's learned feature space rather than a direct biological coordinate system.

Evaluation

The learned representation is evaluated by checking whether biologically related samples are enriched near each other in the embedding space more than would be expected by chance.

This includes comparing neighborhood similarity between replicate wells, agreement between a well's nearest neighbors and its annotated mechanism of action, and the amount of variation explained by technical factors such as plate identity. These are research diagnostics, not evidence of a validated drug discovery model — see Limitations below.

Limitations

This is a proof-of-concept model trained on a subset of available Cell Painting data — not a universal morphology foundation model. Specific limitations to keep in mind:

  • The compound library behind this map is a single fixed reference set (~300 compounds, one plate design) imaged across multiple replicate plates — not the broad chemical diversity used in larger benchmark studies, so results should not be read as a general test of the model's chemistry coverage.
  • The learned representation still shows a measurable plate/batch effect: which plate a well came from is more predictable from the embedding than one might expect from morphology alone.
  • Agreement between a well's neighbors and its annotated MoA is well above random chance, but modest in absolute terms — treat clustering patterns as suggestive, not diagnostic.
  • The 2D UMAP layout is for visualization only. Similarity and neighbor calculations are performed in the full, high-dimensional embedding space and can disagree with what looks closest on the 2D map (see "How to Read This Map").
